So why only watch heat? ↓

the judgment, and the real work

Do not monitor everything. Monitor only what the AI cannot fix itself.

These robots are closed-loop. They see where the object actually is and adapt, so they shrug off most hardware variation: a tilted camera, a little imprecision. Flagging that is noise. Run every candidate fault through two filters, can the policy compensate, and does it actually recur on this hardware, and watch ten candidates collapse to essentially one proven fault. Knowing what not to build is the work.

Ten candidates in, one proven fault out, plus one labeled candidate. That restraint is the point. The burden of proof is on inclusion, every cut is documented, and the architecture adds the next fault the day the fleet data earns it.

Three lower-stakes candidates (comms bus, depth calibration, gearbox backlash) were also evaluated and cut; omitted here for brevity.

what this is

The contribution is the judgment, not the feature count.

I designed and built robot-vitals end to end: an audit that decides which hardware faults are even worth testing on a closed-loop robot and collapses the whole parts list to essentially one that matters, plus a per-run health gate, a patient daily trend detector, a tested Python core, and this live simulation.

It proves that on a closed-loop robot only one fault, an overheating motor, silently corrupts the data and is worth a dedicated test. Knowing what not to build, and refusing to print a single number I cannot defend, is the work. Every threshold here traces to a servo datasheet or is labeled illustrative, pending fleet validation.
